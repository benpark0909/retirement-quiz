Retirement 101

Before you can plan for retirement, you need to understand the building blocks. Click any concept to expand it.

401(k)The Workplace Retirement Account

A 401(k) is a retirement savings account offered by your employer. You contribute a portion of your paycheck before taxes are taken out, lowering your taxable income today. Your money grows tax-deferred until you withdraw it in retirement.

In 2024, you can contribute up to $23,000 per year. Many employers also offer a "match" — if you put in 3%, they add another 3% for free. That's an instant 100% return on matched dollars.

Key takeaway: Always contribute enough to capture your full employer match. It's free money left on the table if you don't.
IRAIndividual Retirement Account

An IRA is a retirement account you open yourself, independent of any employer. It comes in two main flavors: Traditional and Roth. The 2024 contribution limit is $7,000/year ($8,000 if you're 50+).

IRAs offer more investment flexibility than most 401(k) plans — you can pick stocks, ETFs, bonds, and more. They're especially powerful when used alongside a workplace plan.

Key takeaway: Once you've maxed your 401(k) employer match, an IRA is typically your next best savings vehicle.
ROTH vs TRADWhich Account Type Is Right?

Traditional: Contribute pre-tax money (tax break today), pay taxes on withdrawals in retirement. Better if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket later.

Roth: Contribute after-tax money (no break now), but all growth and withdrawals in retirement are 100% tax-free. Better if you expect a higher bracket later.

For most young earners, Roth wins — you're likely in a lower bracket now than in your peak earning years.

Key takeaway: Young earners should lean Roth. Higher earners often benefit more from Traditional accounts.
COMPOUNDCompound Interest — The 8th Wonder

Compound interest means earning returns on your returns. $1,000 at 10% → $1,100. Next year → $1,210. The year after → $1,331. It accelerates every single year, silently and relentlessly.

The real magic is time. $5,000 invested at 22 could grow past $150,000 by retirement. The same amount invested at 42 might only reach $30,000. Time is the most powerful variable in the equation.

Key takeaway: Time in the market beats timing the market. Starting small and early beats starting big and late almost every time.
SOC. SEC.Social Security — Supplement, Not a Plan

Social Security provides monthly income in retirement based on your lifetime earnings. Claim as early as age 62 (reduced) or wait until age 70 for the maximum payout — about 76% more than claiming early.

The average monthly benefit in 2024 is ~$1,907 (~$23,000/year). That covers basic needs in most areas, but it's not a comfortable retirement on its own.

Key takeaway: Treat Social Security as a supplement, not your plan. Your personal savings need to carry the weight.
ALLOC.Asset Allocation & Diversification

Asset allocation means splitting your money between stocks (higher risk, higher reward) and bonds (lower risk, steadier growth). The right mix depends on your age and timeline.

A common rule: subtract your age from 110 — that's roughly the % to hold in stocks. At 25, that's ~85% stocks / 15% bonds. As you age, gradually shift toward bonds to protect what you've built.

Key takeaway: Younger investors can afford more risk. As retirement nears, protecting your balance becomes the priority.

Common Retirement Myths

✕ Myth
"I'll just work until I physically can't."
The Reality
About half of all early retirements are unplanned — caused by health issues, layoffs, or caregiving. Counting on working forever is a risky strategy.
✕ Myth
"Social Security will be enough to live on."
The Reality
The average benefit replaces only ~40% of pre-retirement income. Most planners recommend replacing 70–80% for a comfortable lifestyle.
✕ Myth
"I'll start saving once I'm making more money."
The Reality
Waiting even 10 years to start can cost hundreds of thousands in lost compound growth — even if you save the same total. Time is irreplaceable.
✕ Myth
"Retirement planning is for older people."
The Reality
Your first job is the best time to start. Early small amounts consistently outperform late large ones, thanks to compounding over decades.

About This Project

Why Retirement Literacy Matters

Retirement literacy is one of the most consequential — and most overlooked — forms of financial education we have.

The Problem

Most people learn about retirement far too late. School doesn't teach it. Parents often don't either — not because they don't care, but because they weren't taught themselves. By the time someone realizes they're behind, years of irreplaceable compounding have already passed.

This website was built to close that gap — to make retirement concepts approachable, testable, and actionable for anyone, regardless of financial background.

What You Can Do Today

The best time to start was years ago. The second best time is today. Even if all you can save is $25 a month, the habit starts and the compounding begins immediately.

Use this site to understand the concepts, test where your knowledge stands, and calculate what different saving habits could mean for your future. Small steps taken early are worth far more than large steps taken late.
